Algebraic Formula Size of Component Formulas

Christian Engels
Last updated on May 28, 2020 4 min read

Recently, Chaugule, Kumar, Limaye, Mohapatra, She and Srinivasan released their paper “Shur Polynomials do not have small formulas if the Determinant doesn’t!".

We will explain one of their main lemmas, Lemma 4.2. This post is not meant as a criticizm to the presentation of the paper as it is very understandable written. Rather it is highlight this interesting lemma and help me understand the proof better.

Definitions

The Jacobian matrix $J(f_1,\dots,f_k)$ is defined as $J_{ij}=\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}$.

A set of $n$ variate polynomials ${ q_1,\dots, q_k}\subseteq \mathbf{C}[X]$ is said to satisfy Property $S$, if there exists a $a\in \mathbf{C}_n$ such that

  • For all $i\in [k]$, $q_i(a)=0$,
  • The rank of the Jacobian matri of $q_1,\dots,q_k$ when evaluated at $a$ is equal to its symbolic rank, i.e., rank${\mathbf{C}}(J(q_1,\dots,q_k))(a)=$rank${\mathbf{C}(x)}(J(q_1,\dots,q_k))$.

Lemma

Let ${ q_1,\dots,q_k}\in \mathbf{C}[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ be a set of algebraically independent polynomials which satisfy Property $S$. Let $g\in \mathbf{C}[z_1,\dots,z_k]$ be a homogeneous $k$ variate polynomial of degree equal to $d$. Let $g(q_1,\dots,q_k)\in \mathbf{C}[X]$ has an algebraic formula of size $s$ and depth $\Delta$. Then $g(z_1,\dots,z_k)$ has an algebraic formula of size $O(s^2n)$ and depth $\Delta+O(1)$.

Proof

Prerequisites

Corollary 2.10: Let $P$ have degree at least one.

$P(a+z) \equiv P(a)+\sum_{j=1}^n z_j \cdot \frac{\partial P}{\partial x_j}(a) \mod ^2$. This follows from Taylor expansion.

Proof

  • Let $a$ be the assignment coming from Property $S$, such that $q_1(a)=\dots = q_k(a)=0$ and which keeps the rank equal.
  • Since they are algebraically independent, that means that rank$_{\mathbf{C}}(J(q_1,\dots,q_k)(a))=k$.
  • Corollary 2.10 gives us: $q_i(a+x) \equiv \sum_{j=1}^n x_j \cdot \frac{\partial q_i}{\partial x_j} \mod ^2$, as $q_i(a)=0$.
  • Let us look at $i$th row of the jacobian. This is the vector $(\frac{\partial q_i}{x_1}(a), \dots, \frac{\partial q_i}{\partial x_n}(a))$.
  • The $i$th vectors are all linear independent as as the jacobian has full rank.
  • Thus the vectors given by $\sum_{j=1}^n x_j \frac{\partial q_i}{\partial x_j}(a)$ are linearly independent.
    • Any set of linear forms that is already independent over a smaller field, i.e., $\mod ^2$ will be linearly independent over a larger field, i.e., $\mathbf{C}$.
  • Let us call these vectors $u_i(x)$.
  • Claim 4.3: Let $d$ be the degree of $g(x)$. Then the homogeneous component of degree $d$ of the polynomial $g(q_1,\dots,q_k)$ is equal to $g(u_1,\dots,u_k)$.
  • Claim 4.4: If $g(u_1,\dots,u_k)$ has a formula of size $s'$ and depth $\Delta$ then g(z) has a formula of size at most $s’n$ and depth $\Delta + O(1)$.
  • These two claims finish the proof, as extracting homogeneous components is easy.

Claim 4.3

  • Let $d$ be the degree of $g(x)$. Then the homogeneous component of degree $d$ of the polynomial $g(q_1,\dots,q_k)$ is equal to $g(u_1,\dots,u_k)$.
  • Let $f_1,\dots,f_k$ be polynomials which are zero modulo $^2$ such that $q_i(a+x) = u_i(x) + f_i(x)$.
  • Since $g$ is homogeneous of degree $d$, $g(z)= \sum_{b\in \mathbf{N}^k, d= \sum_{i=1}^k b_i} \alpha_b z^b$.
  • We need to look at the homogeneous components of the polynomial $g(q_1(a+x),\dots,q_k(a+x))= \sum_{b\in \mathbf{N}^k, d= \sum_{i=1}^k b_i} \alpha_b (q_1(a+x))^{b_1} \cdots (q_k(a+x))^{b_k}$.
  • Let us look at the homogeneous components of the following polynomial $\prod_{j=1}^k q_j(a+x)^{b_j} = \prod_{j=1}^k (u_j(x) + f_j(x))^{b_j}$.
  • This is equal to $\prod_{j=1}^k u_j^{b_j}$.
  • Now we know that taking homogeneous components is linear, hence, of the $g(q_1(a+x), \dots, q_k(a+x))$, we can just look at the sums.
  • This gives us that the homogeneous components of $g(q_1(a+x),\dots,q_k(a+x))$ is equal to $\sum_{b\in \mathbf{N}^k, d= \sum_{i=1}^k b_i} \alpha_b \prod_{j=1}^k u_j^{b_j}$.
  • This is equal to the polynomial $g(u_1,\dots,u_k)$.

Claim 4.4

  • If $g(u_1,\dots,u_k)$ has a formula of size $s'$ and depth $\Delta$ then g(z) has a formula of size at most $s’n$ and depth $\Delta + O(1)$.
  • Remember that $u_i$ are linear forms.
  • We will find linear forms such that $u_i(l_1(z),\dots,l_n(z))=z_i$.
  • For this we solve the equation system $U\cdot x^T = z^T$ where $U$ is the $k\times n$ matrix whose $i$th row is $u_i$.
  • Left to show is that the rank of $U$ is $k$.
  • This was shown earlier and follows immediately from the fact that the vectors $v_i=(\frac{\partial q_i}{\partial x_1}(a),\dots, \frac{\partial q_i}{\partial x_n}(a))$ is linearly independent (by the jordan matrix).

Main Takeaways or Things to Remember

  • Checking linear independence over a small subfield might already be enough.
  • Jacobian might help with this.
  • Linearity of homogeneous components can help.

Questions:

  • Can we have less requirements on the lemma for circuits with similar or better size bounds.
