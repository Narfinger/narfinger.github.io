Two Worlds of Doom - A Look at Doom and Doom II

Last updated on May 28, 2020 3 min read Games

Classics of first person shooter. Games books where written about. Games I never played until recently.

I recently got to enjoy my first playthrough of Doom and Doom II and I was suprised to put it midly.

Doom (the first one) is the pure essence of first person shooter. Simple in its design it reminds me a lot of Quake 3 Arena. One game with laser focused design. Shooting monsters, getting loot and finding keys gets second nature. The trusty shotgun always by your side. The space? Random assortment of corridors, hellish nightscapes, undescript space stations and bloody gibs everywhere. Doom is one of the few games which which does not give you any sense of real space. Any corridor, any hall is a mixture between metal grating and fleshy walls and all this is encapsuled in a absurd architecture which never could exist in real life. Combine this with the pixels, the almost undecipherable things you see. Demons, inhabiting this space only through a simple texture. You are never quite sure what they are supposed to be or how they fit into this landscape.

For many people, including me, this is a major charme of Doom. more suprisingly is how the level architecture changes. Remember the days of early 3D games? Suddenly all this was supposed to be a real place. And then you saw these primitive graphics for what they where; places invented by a level designer to mimic a real place.

I got this same feeling playing Doom II. Suddenly, the levels have names. “The Waste Tunnels”, “The Factory”, “Downtown” and “Suburbs”. Gone is the place you could not even understand and in are the real places. Places where you immediatly see that this is not a real place but is trying very hard to be one. Gone is the tone of sheer nonsense, of horror you cannot imagine and in are the “kill all the demons in your suburb. Are you a bad enough dude to do that?” And the disconnect is harsh. Doom was never about a story, never about you being a hero marine to save the earth, phobos or whatever, it was just you, a shotgun and enemies in interesting levels. Now we got “realness”.

You might say that Doom also had these levels. “Chemical Plant”, “Command Center” and so on but I believe these where never meant to be more than an abstract space, despite their names. No architecture in these levels makes any sense and the further you go into Doom, the more these levels lose their real place like a decent into madness.

In fact, Campster makes a similar point in his video. But it seems that Doom II is just missing something.
